Resume

overview

  • first page highlights successes. 2 page max
  • objectives are a waste of time
    • perhaps use a mission-statement
  • show communication preference (email, phone, etc)
  • experiences -- discuss tasks and tools employed
  • preferred tools/languages
  • interests/hobbies section

organization

1-4 are the areas of focus

  1. contact info
  2. objective (optional -- can close doors)
  3. education
  4. experiences & skills
  5. recognition/awards
  6. leadership/organizations

Formatting

  • sans serf >= 10pt
  • page borders at 1 inch ( >= 0.5in at bottom)
  • sparingly use boldface, underlining, etc
  • keep tenses consistent

miscellaneous

  • three C's 1
    1. clear message with clean and simple language
    2. concise statements allowing readers to identify skills, knowledge, and abilities
    3. consistent, easy to read format

getting resumes through Applicant Tracking Systems/Automated Resume Screeners

  • don't focus on keywords in the job description
  • priority keywords: those used more than twice
  • secondary keywords: competitor companies/brand name experience, keyword phrases, notable industry qualifications
  • consult an insider for resume help
  • place keywords throughout all job positions (if possible)
  • use bulleted lists to describe your work
  • make sure resume includes all job requirements

Interview

Researching the Company

  • if a publicly traded company, examine its Annual Report, Proxy Statement, and 10-K (SEC filings and other corporate documents)

Negotiating a HIgher Salary

  • ask for the salary range long before the conversation comes up
  • ask for salary range the first time you meet the recruiter
  • "What is the salary range allocated for this position?"

Questions for Which to Prepare

  • Where do you see yourself in five/ten years?
  • What is your greatest weakness?
    • Choose an irrelevant skill.
    • ex: For example, "Well, accounting really isn't my thing. I understand the basic idea behind book keeping, but I don't really get the nitty-gritty details. Of course, that's also why I'm applying for this job in human resources. I think it leverages my strengths and steers clear of the technical skills that I haven't learned yet…like accounting."
    • "Instead of relying on statements like these, you can clearly answer the question by mentioning a more technical skill that you simply haven’t learned yet and then talk about why that pushes you towards the job you’re applying to get." 
  • Why do you want to work here?

Sample questions to ask

  • How quickly does the company move on ideas (what is the typical time between releases)?
  • What would it take for an engineer to test out an idea on the live site/product?
  • Are groups very distinct or do they work in a more fluid manner?
  • How often do people move around between teams?
  • What is the rough ratio of managers to direct reports?
  • Do they have some sort of formal or informal mentoring program?
  • How much are you going to learn there?
  • Do they offer opportunities to grow and try new things?
  • sources: 2

Things to Avoid

  • Complaining about previous employers.
  • Childcare issues.
  • Financial problems.
  • Your age.
  • Questionable hobbies.
